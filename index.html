Who is a refugee?

noun ref.u.gee \ˌre-fyu̇-ˈjē, ˈre-fyu̇-ˌ\

Someone who has been forced to flee his or her country because of persecution, war, or violence. A refugee has a well-founded fear of persecution for reasons of race, religion, nationality, political opinion or membership in a particular social group.

1. Nearly 1 in 100 people worldwide are displaced from their homes.

2. Successful refugee resettlement and assimilation requires strong connections between urban resources, including education, charity, job development and industry.

3. Ithaca became a sanctuary city in 1985 for Salvadoran and Guatemalan refugees. As of February 2017, Mayor Svante Myrick has amended Ithaca Municipal Code to reinforce Ithaca as a sanctuary city

TRACING REFUGEES TO TOMPKINS COUNTY

The Initial Wave

Refugee resettlement to Ithaca began at the end of the Vietnam War. Cornell University professors, who had built professional and personal ties while visiting Vietnam, were initially responsible with connecting refugees to resources in Ithaca. Given the historical interest in international affairs and the resources available at Cornell, Ithaca expanded from just an educational gateway community to a 'safe haven'. The refugee population also included refugees from all over Southeast Asia, including Cambodia and Loa.

STEPS TO BECOME A REFUGEE

A SHORT LIST

1. Refugees must identify themselves to a UN refugee agency, which collects and reviews documents, performs initial assessment, and conducts initial interview.

Less than 1% of global refugee population will move forward in this process.
2. Applicants are received by a federally-funded resettlement support center.
3. Applicants go through Biographic Security checks.
  • This is the highest level of security checks of any category of traveler to the US
  • This process can be repeated over and over if new information is provided before applicant can proceed
4. Applicants are interviewed by Dept. of Homeland Security.
  • Re-interviews conducted if any additional or concerning information comes up
  • Cases can be placed on hold at this point
5. Applicants go through a round of Biometric Security checks.
  • Fingerprints taken by U.S. government employees
6. Applicants go through medical checks.
7. Applicants are assigned to resettlement location
8. Applicants travel to resettlement locations
